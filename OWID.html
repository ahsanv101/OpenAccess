Quality Analysis


The standards for quality we decided to take into consideration are those of the Open Data Goldbook for Data Managers and Data Holders (provided by European data portal), which are completeness (high level of indicators), cleanness (no empty fields or double entries), accuracy (data fit for its purpose), timeliness (data maintained, up-to-date) and consistency (equal quality of datasets).

Datasets come from reliable sources, such as the World Bank and other United Nations institutions, as well as international scholarly studies. Their data goes hand in hand with their mission to “make the knowledge of the big problems accessible and understandable”, through intuitive and interactive visualizations of the data. There is a full documentation for each dataset, providing information about the data, in the “source” section: it describes the data, the methodology behind its collection and potential limitations, exceptions to its accuracy (it also indicates potential discrepancies between countries). The level of indicators is high enough for its datasets to be considered complete, with topics divided into subtopics that visualize data according to more specific variables. The organization commits itself to regularly update its existing work, with the “best available data”.

Legal Analysis


The datasets we collected derive from different sources that use different licenses. Along with considering the license used by each of our sources, we considered general transparency of the data and the authorities liable for it. These legal aspects come hand in hand with certain purposes that are assigned to each of our datasets.

As made explicit in the footer of the website, the license used by Our World in Data is Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International (CC BY 4.0). The liable supervising authority for legal aspects is the “Global Change Data Lab”, described as the “data controller” for the information that they collect.

Using the Creative Commons license suits the organization’s goal to “build an infrastructure that makes research and data openly available and useful for all”, as mentioned in their “about” page. All of the organization’s work is “made available in its entirety as a public good”: the data (which is available for download) but also the visualizations and texts, which can be freely used for any purpose (provided that the sources and authors are credited). Their visualization code is itself open-sourced under the MIT-license and can be found on GitHub. Some other material, such as data produced by third parties and made available by the OWID, is subject to the license terms from the original third-party authors.

Ethical Analysis


Data ethics is about the responsible and sustainable use of data: is it right for individuals and society? Its main principles concern human rights and privacy protection, but also honest and transparent use of the data. We thus verified if our different datasets were created in a “privacy-by-design” approach, in the entirety of its process (with a particular attention to anonymization), and checked if our datasets are free from discrimination, cognitive bias and prejudice.

Our World in Data gives a particular importance on the ethical aspects of its work, in its mission to encourage the progress of society. All of the information and data that they provide is free of bias (notably through the peer-to-peer review of their scholarly sources), inclusive and anonymized. The purpose of the data collection is clearly stated in the “about” page and the organization is honest as to how it relies “on the work of a global community of scholars” in order to do so.
