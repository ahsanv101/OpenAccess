Quality Analysis


The standards for quality we decided to take into consideration are those of the Open Data Goldbook for Data Managers and Data Holders (provided by European data portal), which are completeness (high level of indicators), cleanness (no empty fields or double entries), accuracy (data fit for its purpose), timeliness (data maintained, up-to-date) and consistency (equal quality of datasets).

UNdata is an entry point to a variety of statistical resources compiled by the United Nations Statistics Division and other international agencies. Their “datamarts” contain more than 60 million data points, an astonishing number which participates in making their data complete, along with their numerous indicators and selection filters (including a “reliability” indicator, for which the grand majority of fields contains the inscription “final figure, complete”). UNdata is maintained by the “Development Data Section”, within the Statistics Division of the UN Secretariat; both the date of the last update and the date of the next update is indicated. As stated in their “conditions of use” page, the United Nations Statistics Division “periodically incorporates, without notice, revisions, updates and improvements to UNdata’s content according to the sources’ availability but undertakes no obligation to do so, timeously or at all”.

Launched as part of the project “Statistics as a Public Good” (2005), UNdata’s datasets are fit for their purpose of providing free access to global statistics and to assist Member Countries in their data dissemination capabilities.

Legal Analysis


The datasets we collected derive from different sources that use different licenses. Along with considering the license used by each of our sources, we considered general transparency of the data and the authorities liable for it. These legal aspects come hand in hand with certain purposes that are assigned to each of our datasets.

In the “terms and conditions of use” page, UNdata states that “all data and metadata provided on UNdata’s website are available free of charge and may be copied freely, duplicated and further distributed provided that UNdata is cited as the reference”. The work of the United Nations Statistics Division (UNSD) is under copyright, as made explicit in the footer of the website. While the liable authority is the UNSD, UNdata clearly states in its “terms and conditions of use” that they have “no liability in the respect of use” of their content (including resulting inaccuracy, error, omission, interruption, deletion, defect, alteration).

As a “web-based data service for the global user community”, being under copyrights law without using a Creative Commons license does not impede their goal “to provide free access to global statistics, to educate users about the importance of statistics for evidence-based policy and decision-making and to assist National Statistical Offices of Member Countries to strengthen their data dissemination capabilities”. Their data can be said to participate in a better symbiosis between science, society and policy.

However, their data is not openly modifiable and reusable with other datasets, under copyright law, which implies that UNSD possesses exclusive rights regarding the use and reproduction of their work. A Creative Commons License would have allowed for a free “repurposing” and “remixing” of their statistics.

Ethical Analysis


Data ethics is about the responsible and sustainable use of data: is it right for individuals and society? Its main principles concern human rights and privacy protection, but also honest and transparent use of the data. We thus verified if our different datasets were created in a “privacy-by-design” approach, in the entirety of its process (with a particular attention to anonymization), and checked if our datasets are free from discrimination, cognitive bias and prejudice.

The UN Statistics Division has a specific page dedicated to its “standards and methods”, with multiple handbooks and guidelines regarding the collection of demographic data. Because the data they represent correspond to very large geographical areas, anonymization is designed by default, and datasets are, as such, free of sensitive, private data. All countries or areas of the world are covered without discrimination - for instance, the State of Palestine is covered as well, even if it became an non-member observer state of the UN, since 2012 only. Depending on the chosen dataset, some countries or states may be missing, because of lack of data.

UNdata is transparent in its approach, methodology and sources: the United Nations compile official statistics produced by countries, makes some estimates and projections, and takes its data from the UN Statistics Division as well as other UN agencies.
