Unemployment rate

Internet use with respect to unemployment


Now, we are interested in finding a relation between the two data graphs above.

The scatterplot of the two variables can make us visualize this relationship:

Download dataset

It comes to our attention that most of the plot points do not show a correlation between the two datasets. There are some countries with low unemployment rates, who also have a low percentage of internet users, and there are also countries where there are high percentages of internet users, but still with low unemployment rates.

The most interesting thing to notice here is that there is a group of countries like Italy, Portugal , Croatia, France, Slovakia etc. who have similar relations, in which their internet user percentages are between 12-16% and their unemployment percentages between 3-6%.

From these two observations we can make an ambivalent deduction on the relationship between these two aspects: while for some European countries, internet use and employment levels seem to be correlated, it is not the case for the globality of European economies. Our second hypothesis, that internet adoption has a determinant role in employment rates, is as such only relatively verified - that is, relative to a certain group of countries in Europe.


Education


Then, we are interested in looking at education levels in Europe. There are many ways to conduct researches in order to measure a country's education rate. For our project, we used a dataset which relies on PISA assessment, which best hints as to the quality of different educational systems. The Program for International Student Assessment (PISA) assessment, is coordinated by the OECD and is the most well known international assessment of learning outcomes. The first PISA study was carried out in 1997 and since then it was repeated every three years. We took the latest PISA score in reading literacy as a scale to measure education quality across European countries.

The heatmap below illustrates that data:

Download dataset

It comes to our attention that countries like Norway, Finland, Sweden etc. show a higher score, indicating that northern European states have a slightly higher PISA score rate, which can be interpreted as northern Europe having a slightly higher education quality, in terms of literacy.


Internet use with respect to education


Now, we will combine the two datasets of education levels and internet use to get a general understanding of the relation between these two aspect in European countries.

The scatterplot below allows us to visualize the relationship between the two datasets:

Download dataset

We can infer from the graph above a clear relationship between the two variables. We see a postive correlation between PISA mean score and the percentage of internet users, as for European countries. Countries like Romania, Bulgaria, Greece etc. show a low PISA score and also a low percentage of internet users. Inversely, countries like Germany, Finland, Ireland etc show a high percentage of internet users and a high PISA score.

This result can be interpreted as the proof of strong link between education levels and the adoption of internet technology. According to our data, larger internet use may lead to higher education levels, or higher education levels may lead to larger internet use: though we cannot infer a causation between one and the other (just a correlation). Nevertheless our third hypothesis - that education levels is rather independant from internet adoption - seems to be proven wrong by our data.

Internet use with respect to education, catered with population


Now that we are taking in population into account we can map that.

The scatterplot below makes us visualize the percentage of internet users in relation with PISA mean scores, where the radius of each data point represents the population:

Download dataset

It is important to note here that PISA mean scores have been normalized on a scale from 1 to 100 whereas population percentages have been normalized on a scale from 1 to 50

One of the most important reasons to take population into account was to understand the percentage of internet users, and whether the population size has some impact on this aspect or not, since the percentage of internet users of a country was calculated in terms of its population size.

We can see from the graph above that population size does not affect the interpretation of our results. There are countries with large populations, who have a high internet user percentage, and there are also countries with smaller populations that still have a high internet user percentage.


Internet use with respect to education and with respect to unemployment


Finally, we can move towards our ultimate question, to look at the relation between internet use, education levels and unemployment rates accross European countries.

After collecting and merging all the relevent data, we produced the graph below:

Download dataset

It is important to note that both the PISA mean score and unemployment rate has been normalized in order to better visualize them

We had already deduced a strong relationship between education levels and internet use, as well as an ambivalent relationship between internet use and unemployment levels. Now, we can integrate each of these three variables with each other, to get the full picture on the relationship between these different aspects of our analysis.

We observe that countries like Sweden, Spain, Latvia etc. have higher PISA score but also higher unemployment rate. However, there are also countries like Finland, Slovania, Germany, Poland etc. who have a relatively high PISA score, but that have lower unemployment rate. This goes to show that unemployment rate is not determined by education levels, according to our data - that is, we can infer a non-causation between these two aspects, despite that we assumed the contrary in our first hypothesis.
